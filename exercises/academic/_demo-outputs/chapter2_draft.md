# Chapter 2: Literature Review

## 2.1 AI Adoption Literature

The question of why organisations adopt artificial intelligence has shifted from "whether" to "how fast" — yet the factors driving adoption in small and medium enterprises remain distinct from those in large corporations. [needs citation: Chen AI adoption UK SMEs] found that among 287 UK SMEs, competitive pressure is the strongest predictor of AI tool adoption (β=0.42, p<0.001), outweighing cost considerations — only 34% cited cost as a primary barrier. This finding challenges earlier narratives that framed AI adoption primarily as a capital expenditure decision [needs citation: Bughin AI frontier]. The shift toward API-based, pay-per-use AI services has fundamentally restructured the adoption equation from a capital question to an operational one [needs citation: Chen AI adoption UK SMEs].

However, adoption rates alone tell an incomplete story. Survey data from 100 KCL postgraduate students reveal that even among heavy AI users (freq_use_ai ≥ 4, n=50), trust in AI outputs remains low (trust_ai_output mean 2.88/5), nearly identical to infrequent users (mean 2.80/5, n=50). This suggests that increased tool use does not automatically translate into increased confidence in the tool's reliability — a micro-level echo of the adoption-without-capability pattern that [needs citation: Hoffmann skills mismatch] identified at the organisational level.

## 2.2 Capability-Based View of Digital Transformation

Digital transformation initiatives fail not because organisations lack technology, but because they lack the capabilities to deploy technology effectively. [needs citation: Hoffmann skills mismatch] surveyed 412 managers across four sectors and found a striking asymmetry: technical skill depth correlates weakly with transformation success (r=0.18), while what they term "judgement skills" — the ability to scope use cases, evaluate AI outputs, and recognise when to override automated recommendations — correlates strongly (r=0.61). Yet most surveyed firms invest eight times more in technical training than in judgement training.

This capability gap manifests clearly in educational settings. Among 100 KCL postgraduate students surveyed, 9% explicitly reported dependency concerns ("I rely on it too much for first drafts and my own writing has gotten lazy"), while 5% struggled to locate the boundary between legitimate assistance and academic dishonesty ("Hard to know where to draw the line between help and doing it for me"). These qualitative responses parallel [needs citation: Hoffmann skills mismatch]'s finding that younger managers report higher confidence in AI use but lower accuracy in identifying flawed outputs — suggesting that the judgement gap is not unique to corporate contexts but extends to academic knowledge work.

The framework of "judgement-augmented automation" proposed by [needs citation: Hoffmann skills mismatch] offers a theoretical bridge between tool adoption and capability building. Rather than asking whether a student or manager *uses* AI, the more productive question is whether they have developed the evaluative capacity to distinguish reliable from unreliable outputs — and whether they act on that distinction.

## 2.3 Platform Strategy and AI

The platform perspective adds a structural dimension to the adoption-capability discussion. [needs citation: Almeida Yamamoto platform strategy] conducted a longitudinal study of three digital platforms and found that generative AI shifts platforms from "matchmakers" to "co-producers" — they now actively generate content rather than merely routing it. This co-producer role creates new liability exposure, particularly around copyright and accuracy, and migrates value capture upstream: platforms charge for generation, not just transactions.

For SMEs operating on or through such platforms, this structural shift carries practical implications. Producer-side users report mixed sentiment toward AI integration — 58% see it as opportunity, 42% as threat [needs citation: Almeida Yamamoto platform strategy]. This ambivalence mirrors the tension identified in the survey data: students use AI tools extensively (freq_use_ai mean 3.54/5, n=100) but harbour persistent integrity concerns (concern_integrity mean 3.48/5, n=100), with the correlation between the two being negligible (r = -0.039). The concern is structural, not usage-dependent — nearly half of both high-frequency and low-frequency users report elevated concern (44% vs 48% with concern ≥ 4).

## 2.4 The Gap: From Tool Use to Capability Building

The existing literature offers three lenses — adoption drivers, organisational capabilities, and platform dynamics — but almost none connects them into a coherent account of how SMEs move from *using* AI tools to *building* durable capabilities around them [needs citation: Chen AI adoption UK SMEs]. [needs citation: Chen AI adoption UK SMEs] documents the adoption decision but not its downstream consequences. [needs citation: Hoffmann skills mismatch] identifies the capability gap but studies large enterprises, not SMEs. [needs citation: Almeida Yamamoto platform strategy] captures the platform-level reconfiguration but does not examine how individual firms respond.

This dissertation aims to bridge that gap by investigating the transition mechanism: what determines whether AI tool adoption in UK SMEs leads to genuine capability building, or remains at the level of ad-hoc productivity enhancement? The proposed three-stage model — Tool Use → Workflow Integration → Capability Building — draws on [needs citation: Hoffmann skills mismatch]'s judgement-augmented automation framework while grounding it in the SME context that [needs citation: Chen AI adoption UK SMEs] has mapped.
